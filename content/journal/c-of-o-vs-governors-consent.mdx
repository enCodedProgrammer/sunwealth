A Certificate of Occupancy — the C of O — is the document the Governor of Lagos State signs to confirm that a person holds a 99-year leasehold over a specific parcel of land. It is the strongest form of private title for most properties in Lagos. Without one, the land is either still held in the old family owner's name, still under government acquisition, or held under an Excision — none of which give a buyer the same certainty as a C of O.

Governor's Consent is a separate document, and this is where most Lagos buyers get caught out. The first time a parcel is titled, you get a C of O. **Every time that titled land is sold to a new owner after that, the transfer requires Governor's Consent**. Without Governor's Consent, the deed of assignment between buyer and seller is not perfected, even if the money has changed hands and the keys have been handed over. It is a real, enforceable gap.

Practically, this means:

- If you're buying raw land for the first time from a family or community, you want a C of O.
- If you're buying a property from someone who already holds a C of O, you want both the original C of O **and** the Governor's Consent confirming the transfer to you.
- If you're buying into a new-build estate, ask whether the estate itself holds a C of O, and whether your specific unit will have a Governor's Consent perfected in your name.
