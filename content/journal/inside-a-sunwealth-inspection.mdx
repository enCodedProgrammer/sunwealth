A two-bedroom apartment in Ikoyi came across our desk last month. The owner wanted it listed the same week. We told them we couldn't, not because we doubted the property, but because we hadn't been inside it yet. That's the rule. No inspection, no listing.

Here is what that inspection looked like.

**09:50 WAT**: Our agent arrives at the estate gate with the vendor's instruction letter, a tape measure, a camera, and a printed copy of the property's C of O reference. Security phones the on-site manager, who confirms the visit.

**10:00**: Walk-through begins from the entrance. The agent photographs each room from two or three angles — not for the listing yet, just for the internal verification record. Every photograph is timestamped and geotagged.

**10:25**: Tape measurements on each room. The vendor's brief said the master bedroom was 18 square metres. Our agent measured 16.4 square metres. That's not a scandal — vendor briefs often round — but it's the difference between an accurate listing and a disappointed buyer on handover day.

**10:45**: The agent calls the building manager to confirm service charge, generator runtime, and any outstanding levies. One outstanding levy of ₦180,000 for last year's common-area repainting. Logged.

**11:10**: The C of O reference is sent to our back office. By early afternoon, a registry search confirms the document is on file and unencumbered.

**14:20**: The listing goes into draft. Photographs are retaken the following day in better light, by our photographer rather than the agent. Once those land and the registry search is clean, the listing publishes.

That's the sequence. It takes a day, sometimes two. The reason it takes that long is the same reason our inventory is smaller than any aggregator's: we publish what we have seen, measured and verified. And nothing else.
